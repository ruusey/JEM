							<h1>How It Started</h1>
							<h1>
								<small>Historically, Native Americans were extremely
									content and even wealthy in a sense...</small>
							</h1>
						</div>
						<br> <br>
						<p class="lead">
							<strong>The Early Days</strong>
						</p>
						<p style="text-align: left;">After the arrival of Christopher
							Columbus in the New World in 1492, Native Americans would find
							their rights and resources slowly stripped from their possession.
							Forced cultural apporopriation and marginalization would continue
							to displace and ostracize Native American communities from the
							rest of Western society as history progressed.</p>
						<hr>
						<p class="lead">
							<strong>The Dawes Act (1887)</strong>
						</p>

						<p style="text-align: left;">The objectives of the Dawes Act
							were to lift the Native Americans out of poverty, to stimulate
							assimilation of them into mainstream American society, and to
							transfer lands under Indian control to white settlers. The act
							would grant plots of land to Indian families in an attempt to
							make Native Americans good Jeffersonian yeoman farmers.</p>
						<p style="text-align: left;">While the Dawes Act may have
							served to benefit Native Americans superficially, its primary
							goal was actually to severely reduce the amount of land that
							tribes held.</p>
						<hr>
						<p class="lead">
							<strong>Moving Forward</strong>
						</p>
						<p style="text-align: left;">Unfortunately the robbery of
							Native American lands and resources is only half of the story. As
							white settlement continued to expand into Indian territories
							through the 1800s, individuals and government systems began
							developing means by which the great values of Americanism might
							be instilled upon the Native peoples in an attempt to minimize
							settler-Indian conflict</p>
						<p style="text-align: left;">The disenfranchisement of Native
							Americans as a whole was expidited by the rapid Western expansion
							of the late 1800s and 1900s. Native American communities are now
							extremely scattered and becoming less and less visible in the
							eyes of modern Western culture.</p>

									<p>Tribal Sovereignty is the inherent authority of

										the United States of America. Essentially this means tribes
										are independent nations within the United States.</p>
